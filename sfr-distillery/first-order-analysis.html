Section 02   Strategic Insights
Acquisition Channel
POS-Dominant Acquisition (76%)
Three in four new customers are walking into a physical location — a tasting room or retail outlet — before they ever receive an email. By the time we have their address, they've already held the bottle, tasted the product, and made a purchase. This is a fundamentally different starting point than a cold online acquisition.
Implication: Welcome flows must lead with warmth and craft story, not a discount offer. Discounting a customer who already bought at full POS price is a margin leak and signals poor personalization. The welcome series should deepen the brand relationship, not incentivize a second purchase they might have made anyway.
Data Quality Risk
Email Capture Quality: 8% Opt-in Rate
Both SMS and marketing opt-in rates sit at exactly 8% — 4 customers out of 50. The remaining 92% are in the Klaviyo profile list but have not consented to marketing. This is a structural problem that limits lifecycle revenue from day one. Flows can be built, but the audience to send them to is almost nonexistent without tackling opt-in first.
Implication: Before any lifecycle flow can generate meaningful revenue, an opt-in re-engagement sequence is required. This should be one of the first things built — likely a transactional-style email that directs customers to opt in, framed around exclusive content or early access rather than a discount.
Acquisition Economics
Discount Dependency (52%)
Half of all first-time customers used a discount code on their first purchase. Most of these appear to be TIS-prefixed codes consistent with Toki-style referral/POS attribution codes, suggesting a systematic incentive layer baked into the POS acquisition channel. Two customers explicitly show "Toki code" as the discount source.
Implication: Worth investigating whether discounted first-order customers have lower repeat purchase rates vs non-discounted ones. If discounted cohorts don't repeat, the acquisition economics look much worse than the headline CAC suggests. This is a cohort analysis to build once there's 90+ days of repeat data.
Revenue Opportunity
Low AOV Signals High Cross-Sell Upside
The $70.93 average order value reflects a customer base that is mostly buying single SKUs — SFR Whisky at $39.95–$67.93, individual liqueurs at $30–$45. The catalog has meaningful breadth (gin, vodka, brandy, herbal liqueurs, sampler sets), but first-order customers are rarely discovering more than one product.
Implication: Post-purchase cross-sell is one of the highest-leverage flows available here. A customer who buys SFR Whisky should be introduced to the Trickster's Orange Liqueur or Monk's Secret within 7 days. Pair products strategically — whisky buyers → sampler set or gin; liqueur buyers → Apple Brandy or Maple Eau-de-vie.
Context
This Is the Baseline — Flows Don't Exist Yet
The data spans primarily April–June 2026. SFR is in early-stage DTC growth — email and lifecycle automation is either nonexistent or in very early form. The 50 customers in this dataset represent the foundation we're building from. There is no existing welcome series, post-purchase flow, or winback sequence generating revenue in the background. Everything we build from here is additive.
Implication: The opportunity is large precisely because nothing is automated yet. Even a modest welcome series and post-purchase flow would generate revenue that currently goes completely uncaptured. Start with high-confidence, high-impact flows — welcome + post-purchase — before building anything sophisticated.

Section 05   Recommended Next Steps
What This Data Tells Us to Build First
1
Welcome Flow — POS-Triggered, Warmth-First
76% of new customers are coming from physical retail. The welcome sequence should open with the brand story, the craft, the people behind it — not a discount. They've already bought. Earn the next one through relationship, not incentive.
2
Opt-in Capture Sequence
92% of captured profiles haven't consented to marketing. Without solving this, lifecycle revenue is structurally capped. Build a re-consent sequence framed around value — early access, exclusive content, or first-look on new releases — not a discount pitch.
3
Post-Purchase Cross-Sell (7-Day Window)
Most first orders are single-SKU at $35–$70. The catalog depth is there — customers just aren't seeing it. A product-pairing email within 7 days of first purchase (whisky → liqueurs, gin → sampler set) is high-conversion, low-effort lift.
4
First-Purchase Winback — 60-Day Re-engagement
Given there's no active lifecycle program, a large share of these 50 customers have likely gone silent. A winback sequence at 60 days post-purchase — with a compelling reason to return (seasonal release, craft story, new product) — should run immediately.
5
Discount-Free Acquisition Test
52% of first orders used a discount code. The hypothesis to test: do discounted first-order customers have lower LTV than non-discounted ones? Track the two cohorts over 90 days. If the data confirms it, that's a case to reduce discount dependency at POS acquisition.
