Act 01  /  The scale-up

First, they
opened the taps.

Volume was the obvious move. OhSnap sent 76 campaigns this half versus 60 a year ago, and each landed in a much larger audience — 37.8 million sends against 22.1 million. That alone would lift revenue. But volume is the cheap lever, and it usually costs you the list.

It didn't. Which is where the story gets interesting.

Act 03  /  The other half

Flows quietly run
half the program.

Automated flows drive nearly half of email revenue — $1.38M, or 46% of the total, against 54% from campaigns. They grew +68% year over year too. The twist isn't that flows are weak; it's that their share slipped from 58% to 46%, because campaigns grew even faster this half. And Klaviyo as a whole drives about 25% of total revenue.

◆ How flow revenue is counted here

Flow revenue is summed across all message statuses, not just currently-live ones. The export's status is a snapshot of today's flow library, so flows OhSnap rebuilt and renamed in 2026 (the JD- and LDM- versions) now sit under draft or manual yet still earned in-period — counting live-only would understate flows by roughly 4×. Because several 2026-built flows show $0 in the 2025 window, the +68% flow YoY is, if anything, conservative.

Act 04  /  Where the runway is

Act 06  /  How repeat actually happens

Three champions,
one restart pattern.

Aggregate rates hide the real rhythm. Here are three "Champions" from the lifecycle data, each on their own card, every order plotted in sequence. The shape repeats: purchases arrive in bursts, years apart, and the orders that restart the clock are almost always sale- or offer-triggered. Nodes are evenly spaced so no order hides behind another — the gap between them shows the real days elapsed. Hover any order for the full detail.

Matt M. Champion · since 2019
4 orders · $233 LTV · longest gap 1,982d
A day-one customer who bought a single grip in December 2019, then went silent for five and a half years. A Memorial Day offer finally pulled him back in 2025 — and since returning he's ordered three more times in twelve months.
What it tells us

Dormant isn't dead. One well-timed sale reawakened a five-year-cold buyer and unlocked three orders in a year. A winback with the right offer can restart customers you'd written off.

Shawn F. Champion · since 2020
5 orders · $273 LTV · longest gap 1,919d
An early adopter who ordered three times in 2020–21, then disappeared for over five years. His comeback wasn't a discount — it was a product: a $164 MCON + Dock order, his largest ever, placed on zero email clicks. He reordered two months later.
What it tells us

Not every comeback is offer-driven. A new flagship product (MCON) reactivated a lapsed buyer that discounts never reached. Launches and restocks are their own winback lever — and post-purchase should catch him now that he's active again.

Hadrian H. Champion · since 2020
9 orders · $629 LTV · longest gap 681d
The most engaged of the three — nine orders and $629 LTV over six years. Nearly every purchase rides a promo (Snap10, Labor Day, Cyber Monday, Mother's Day), and he upgrades to the newest grip generation each cycle.
What it tells us

The loyalist runs on the calendar. He upgrades every product generation and answers holiday promos like clockwork — the ideal profile for a VIP / early-access track and margin-aware offers rather than deep discounts.

The lesson lands the same way three times: repeat revenue is sporadic and offer-triggered. Matt reappears on a Memorial Day code after 5+ years dark; Shawn's biggest-ever order is an MCON comeback on zero clicks; Hadrian buys on Cyber Monday, BFCM and Mother's Day like clockwork. Each one maps to a lane in the flow playbook above — Matt is the winback offer, Shawn is post-purchase catching a reactivated buyer, Hadrian is the loyalist track (margin-aware, early-access, no deep discount needed). Persona-relevant product recs plus a well-timed comeback code — your SNAP20 — is the mechanism that turns these gaps into orders.

The verdict

What to keep,
watch, and chase.

Step back and the half tells one story. The campaign engine is firing — more volume, more efficient, healthier per send — and flows are a mature ~46% of revenue, carried by Welcome and abandonment. But both are winning the first sale. The prize hiding in plain sight is the second one: 71% of customers never come back, post-purchase and winback are the thinnest flows in the program, and the personas and journeys show exactly how to reach them. Fix the second order and everything else compounds.
